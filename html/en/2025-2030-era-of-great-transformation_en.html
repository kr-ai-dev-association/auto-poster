2025-2030 Era of Great Transformation: A New Economic Paradigm

1. Introduction: The End of the "Great Moderation" and the Rise of the Polycrisis

The macroeconomic assumptions that have governed the global economy for the past 40 years are fundamentally shifting. From the mid-1980s until the 2020 pandemic, the world enjoyed the "Great Moderation," characterized by low inflation, stable interest rates, and a relatively peaceful geopolitical environment. During this period, capital was cheap, labor was infinitely supplied through globalization, and technology acted as a constant deflationary pressure. However, in-depth analysis confirms that this golden age has definitively ended.

We have now entered a phase known as the "Polycrisis." This refers to a phenomenon where multiple shocks—structural shifts in monetary policy, disruptive technological innovation (AI), and irreversible demographic changes—interact and amplify one another. For South Korea, an export-dependent nation with the world's fastest-aging population, these changes represent not a mere cyclical downturn, but a structural crisis threatening its very survival.

2. Structural Shift in Monetary Policy: The "Higher for Longer" Era

2.1 The End of Zero Rates and the Rise of the Neutral Rate

Since the 2008 global financial crisis, the world economy has been sustained by unconventional policies like Zero Interest Rate Policy (ZIRP) and Quantitative Easing (QE). However, the "Fed Put"—the belief that central banks will always lower rates at the first sign of trouble—is no longer valid. The core shift is the rise of the Neutral Rate ($r^*$), the interest rate that neither stimulates nor restrains the economy. Due to supply chain costs from de-globalization, massive capital demand for the "Green Transition," and expanding fiscal deficits, $r^*$ has been adjusted upward.

2.2 The Mechanism of Sticky Inflation

While "Goods Inflation" caused by pandemic supply chains has eased, "Services Inflation" remains entrenched. The core of service prices is wages. The US labor market faces a structural shortage, risking a "Wage-Price Spiral." Lowering inflation from 9% to 4% was relatively easy as supply chains normalized, but the "Last Mile"—moving from 3-4% down to the 2% target—requires painful demand suppression. Consequently, we must accept high interest rates as the "New Normal."

2.3 Global Interest Rate Differentials and the Currency Trilemma

High US rates exert immense pressure on emerging economies. While the US economy can withstand 5% rates due to tech innovation, South Korea, burdened by household debt, struggles to keep pace. This creates a gap between the Bank of Korea and the Fed, leading to capital flight toward high-yield US Treasuries. This depreciates the Won, causing "Imported Inflation." Korea is trapped in a Trilemma: needing to lower rates for the domestic economy but forced to keep them high to defend the currency.

3. Technological Transformation: AI Revolution and Semiconductor Wars

3.1 Generative AI: A Productivity Revolution

Unlike the Dot-com bubble, the current AI boom is based on massive Capital Expenditure (CapEx) and real productivity gains. AI is becoming an essential "General Purpose Technology," transforming intellectual labor from coding to legal analysis. This will be the primary engine of global growth over the next decade.

3.2 The HBM War in the Semiconductor Cycle

The physical backbone of AI is the GPU, dominated by NVIDIA. However, for Korea, the key is High Bandwidth Memory (HBM). Unlike traditional commodity memory, HBM is an "order-based" industry tailored to specific customers.

  • SK Hynix: Emerged as NVIDIA's key partner through early investment in HBM.
  • Samsung Electronics: Struggling due to late entry into the HBM market and internal bureaucratic friction. This highlights that "Agility" is now more important than "Economies of Scale."

4. The Korean Crisis: Demographic Collapse and Structural Decay

4.1 The "0.6" Shock: Arithmetic Extinction

A total fertility rate of 0.6 is unprecedented outside of war or plague. This is a leading indicator of systemic collapse. A shrinking working-age population will drive potential growth toward 0% or negative territory, leading to "Industrial Hollow-out" as businesses flee a shrinking domestic market.

4.2 Pension Depletion and Generational Conflict

The National Pension Service is expected to be depleted by 2055. Under a 0.6 fertility rate, the burden on future generations becomes mathematically impossible, requiring them to pay over 30% of their income in insurance premiums. This sets the stage for extreme social conflict between generations.

5. Real Estate Bubbles and the Debt Fuse

The weakest link in the Korean economy is Real Estate Project Financing (PF). As interest rates spiked and construction costs soared, many development sites became non-viable. If these fail, it could trigger a credit crunch. Additionally, the unique Jeonse system (private lending for housing) acts as a hidden debt fuse; if housing prices fall further, the liquidity risk could destabilize the entire financial system.

6. Scenarios and Strategy (2025-2030)

  • Scenario A (20%): Soft Landing. AI exports explode, and bold immigration/labor reforms stabilize growth.
  • Scenario B (50%): Japanification. Long-term stagnation. Export success fails to "trickle down," and the population continues to shrink.
  • Scenario C (30%): Polycrisis. Geopolitical shocks trigger oil price spikes, causing real estate PF and household debt to explode simultaneously—a "Second IMF Crisis."

6.2 Strategic Recommendations

For Investors: Shift away from the "Real Estate Infallibility" myth. Diversify into dollar-denominated assets (US Treasuries, Tech stocks) to hedge against Won depreciation.

For Policy: Establish an Immigration Agency to mitigate labor shortages. Implement "Pay more, get less" pension reforms immediately. Expand AI competitiveness beyond manufacturing into software and services.

7. Conclusion: Adapt or Perish

The rules of the world have changed: the cost of money is high, labor is scarce, and AI is both a threat and the only savior. Korea is currently a "Nutcracker," squeezed by US technology and Chinese volume. The only solution is a Productivity Revolution. We must pivot to a high-value economy where AI and robotics compensate for the shrinking population. Now is the time for cold-eyed realism and swift adaptation.
