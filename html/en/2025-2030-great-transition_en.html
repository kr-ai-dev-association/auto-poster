2025–2030 The Era of Great Transition: A New Economic Paradigm of Monetary Tightening, Technological Revolution, and Demographic Collapse

1. Introduction: The End of the 'Great Moderation' and the Dawn of the Polycrisis

The macroeconomic assumptions that have dominated the global economy for the past 40 years are being fundamentally shaken. From the mid-1980s until the eve of the 2020 pandemic, the world enjoyed the "Great Moderation"—a period characterized by low inflation, stable interest rates, and a relatively peaceful geopolitical environment. During this time, capital was cheap, labor was infinitely supplied through globalization, and technology acted as a persistent deflationary force. However, according to in-depth analysis, this golden era has definitively ended.

We have now entered a phase known as a "Polycrisis." This signifies a situation where, rather than a single economic shock, multiple forces—structural shifts in monetary policy, disruptive technological innovation (AI), and irreversible demographic changes—interact and amplify one another. For the South Korean economy, which is highly dependent on exports and faces the fastest population aging in the world, these changes represent not a mere cyclical downturn, but a structural crisis threatening its very survival.

2. Structural Shift in Monetary Policy: The Era of "Higher for Longer"

2.1 The End of Cheap Money and the Rise of the Neutral Rate

Since the 2008 global financial crisis, the global economy has been sustained by unconventional monetary policies like Zero Interest Rate Policy (ZIRP) and Quantitative Easing (QE). Market participants grew accustomed to this decade-long "liquidity party" and came to believe in the "Fed Put"—the idea that central banks would immediately cut rates at the first sign of a slowdown. However, this expectation is no longer valid.

The current high-interest-rate environment led by the U.S. Federal Reserve is not a temporary phenomenon but a structural shift reflecting changes in the economy's fundamental health. The core change is the rise of the Neutral Rate ($r^*$). The neutral rate is the interest rate at which the economy is at full employment and stable inflation—neither overheated nor depressed. While this rate was very low in the past, supply chain costs from de-globalization, massive capital demand for the Green Transition, and expanding government fiscal deficits have pushed the neutral rate higher.

2.2 The Mechanism of Sticky Inflation

The fundamental reason the Fed cannot easily lower rates is the changing nature of inflation. While "goods inflation" caused by pandemic-era supply chain disruptions has subsided, "services inflation" has become entrenched. The core of service pricing is wages. The U.S. labor market continues to face wage pressure due to structural labor shortages (the "Great Resignation," reduced immigration, etc.). Once wages rise, they rarely fall, creating a risk of a "Wage-Price Spiral."

Furthermore, the "Last Mile" of inflation control is proving difficult. Reducing inflation from 9% to 4% was relatively easy as supply chains normalized, but moving from 4% to the 2% target requires painful demand suppression. This incentivizes the Fed to maintain high rates even at the risk of recession. Thus, the "rapid and explosive rate cuts" expected by markets are unlikely; we must accept a world where higher rates are the "New Normal."

2.3 Global Interest Rate Gaps and the Exchange Rate Dilemma

High U.S. interest rates exert immense pressure on emerging economies, including South Korea. While the U.S. economy can withstand 5% rates due to technological innovation and strong consumption, the Korean economy, burdened by household debt, struggles to keep up. This leads to a record-high or long-term gap between the Bank of Korea's and the Fed's base rates.

This gap inevitably causes exchange rate instability. Capital naturally flows toward higher yields. If U.S. Treasuries—the world's safest asset—offer 4-5% returns, the incentive to invest in Korean assets diminishes. This results in the depreciation of the Won, which brings the "boomerang effect" of imported inflation for Korea, a nation heavily dependent on imported energy and food. Consequently, the Bank of Korea is trapped in a "Trilemma," forced to maintain high rates to prevent capital flight and defend the currency, even as the domestic economy cools.

3. Technological Transformation: The AI Revolution and Semiconductor Wars

3.1 Generative AI: A Productivity Revolution, Not Just a Theme

The second pillar of change is the explosive growth of Artificial Intelligence (AI), specifically Generative AI. This trend differs qualitatively from the Dot-com bubble or the Metaverse hype. While previous themes bet on future possibilities, the current AI boom is built on concrete capital expenditure (CapEx) and actual productivity gains by corporations.

Hyperscalers like Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta are pouring astronomical sums into AI infrastructure. AI has become a necessity for survival. In intellectual labor sectors—coding, design, legal analysis, customer service—AI is moving beyond a "Copilot" to a potential replacement. As a "General Purpose Technology," AI will be the primary driver of global economic growth over the next decade.

3.2 The Semiconductor Supercycle and the HBM War

The physical foundation of the AI revolution is the semiconductor, with NVIDIA at the center. NVIDIA's GPUs have become the standard for AI computation, and its software ecosystem (CUDA) forms a powerful moat. For the Korean economy, the more critical shift is in the High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) market. Unlike the traditional commodity-based memory market of mass production, HBM is an "order-based industry" tailored to specific customer needs.

3.2.1 The Crisis of Samsung Electronics and the Leap of SK Hynix

The current semiconductor landscape shows a sharp divergence between Korean giants. SK Hynix, formerly the perpetual second-place player, invested preemptively in HBM and emerged as NVIDIA's key partner. Conversely, Samsung Electronics, the traditional memory powerhouse, is struggling after missing the window for HBM market entry.

3.3 Power Shortages and the Rise of Energy Security

The expansion of AI data centers requires massive electricity. Training and running Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT consumes dozens of times more power than traditional search engines. This directly conflicts with global "Net Zero" goals. Consequently, the growth of AI demands a reorganization of the energy industry. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) are gaining attention as stable, 24/7 power sources, presenting a new opportunity for Korea, a leader in nuclear technology.

4. Korea's Crisis: The Population Cliff and Structural Decay

4.1 The '0.6' Shock: The Stage of Arithmetic Extinction

The most severe issue is the collapse of South Korea's demographic structure. A total fertility rate in the 0.6 range is lower than rates seen during the Black Death or major wars. This is a leading indicator of systemic collapse. A shrinking population leads to a reduced labor supply and a contracting domestic market, pushing potential growth rates toward 0% or into negative territory.

4.2 Pension Depletion and Intergenerational Conflict

The fiscal impact of low birth rates is most immediate in the National Pension Service. The current system relies on younger generations supporting the current retirees. With a 0.6 fertility rate, the burden on future generations becomes mathematically impossible. Projections suggest the fund will be depleted by 2055, after which future generations might have to pay over 30% of their income in insurance premiums just to sustain the system.

4.3 Socio-Economic Drivers: Competitive Pressure and Real Estate

Why are Koreans not having children? The analysis points to the high-density competitive culture and real estate issues rather than a simple lack of subsidies. High housing costs in the Seoul metropolitan area and the astronomical cost of private education (Hagwons) force a "rational but collectively suicidal" strategy: skipping marriage or limiting children to maximize the "success probability" of a single child.

5. Real Estate Bubble and Debt Triggers: PF Crisis and Household Debt

5.1 The Fragility of Real Estate Project Financing (PF)

The weakest link in the Korean economy is real estate PF. During the low-interest-rate era, non-banking financial institutions (securities firms, savings banks) lent heavily to development projects. With rising rates and soaring construction costs, many of these sites are now at risk of default. This is a time bomb that could trigger a chain of bankruptcies in the construction sector and a credit crunch in the financial system.

5.2 The Leverage Risk of the Jeonse System

Korea's unique Jeonse (lump-sum deposit) system is essentially private lending. Homeowners borrow large sums from tenants interest-free to buy properties (gap investment). If house prices drop, this structure collapses. This hidden private debt is a massive trigger for systemic liquidity crises if the real estate market continues to cool.

6. Future Scenarios and Strategic Responses (2025–2030)

6.1 Three Scenario Analysis

  • Scenario A: Soft Landing and Tech Leap (20% Probability) – U.S. rates decline moderately, AI semiconductor exports explode, and bold immigration/labor reforms stabilize the potential growth rate.
  • Scenario B: Long-term Stagnation/Japanification (50% Probability) – Prolonged high rates stifle consumption. Semiconductors do well, but the "trickle-down" effect is missing. Korea enters a period of low growth and low inflation similar to Japan's "Lost Decades."
  • Scenario C: Polycrisis Outbreak (30% Probability) – Geopolitical shocks (Taiwan Strait, Middle East) cause oil prices to spike. Real estate PF and household debt explode, leading to a structural adjustment period akin to the 1997 IMF crisis.

6.2 Strategic Recommendations

For Investors: Diversify globally. Move away from the myth of "unfailing domestic real estate." Increase the proportion of dollar assets (U.S. Treasuries, Tech stocks) to hedge against Won depreciation.

For Policy Makers: Establish an Immigration Agency to bring in skilled foreign labor. Implement "pay more, receive less" pension reforms immediately. Foster an AI software ecosystem beyond hardware manufacturing through deregulation.

7. Conclusion: Adapt or Perish

The world after 2024 operates by entirely different rules. The cost of money (interest rates) is high, labor is scarce, and technology (AI) is both a threat and the only savior. South Korea is in a "Nutcracker" position—competing with the U.S. in technology and being chased by China in volume and price. However, the key to overcoming this crisis lies in a Productivity Revolution. Improving per-capita productivity through AI and robotics is the only way to break through the demographic and structural traps. Now is the time for cold-eyed realism and swift action.
